Electricians Miss Calls Mid-Job. AI Catches Them.

Electricians can't answer their phones while working inside a live panel. The emergency call that came in while both hands were occupied just went to voicemail — and the homeowner moved on to the next Google result before the job was done. I mapped where those calls go and what a properly configured AI response system actually looks like for a two-person electrical shop.

The call comes in at 2pm on a Tuesday. A homeowner has a bathroom outlet that sparked, tripped the GFCI, and now half the kitchen is dead. They Google "electrician near me," call the first result, get voicemail, and immediately call the second result.

The first electrician was inside a service panel with a voltage tester in each hand. They weren't ignoring the phone — they literally couldn't pick it up without stopping a job that requires both hands and sustained focus. By the time they surfaced 40 minutes later and called back, the homeowner had already booked someone else.

I've been mapping where emergency electrical calls actually land during a typical workday, and the answer is mostly voicemail. Not because electricians are bad at customer service, but because the work itself makes answering impossible during significant stretches of the day.

The shops that are winning on emergency service aren't picking up faster. They're responding faster — and that's a very different problem.
